Bitcoin Didn’t Crash. Liquidity Did.

Nov-2025 Macro Bitcoin Japanese Yields
Key focal point

Bitcoin’s sudden drop toward $80k wasn’t a “crypto crash.” It was a global liquidity shock triggered by a spike in Japanese Government Bond yields after Japan announced a $135bn stimulus package.

TL;DR

Japan’s new stimulus pushed JGB yields sharply higher. Higher yields destabilised the yen carry trade—a core source of global leveraged liquidity. As the carry trade unwound, risk assets were dumped mechanically, and Bitcoin—being the most liquidity-sensitive asset on the planet—reacted first and fastest.

Chain of events

Japan fires a $135bn fiscal package into the system. Markets instantly price in more JGB issuance and a steeper yield curve. JGB yields spike. That sounds like an obscure footnote, but it’s a brick through the window of global funding markets.

The yen carry trade depends on low Japanese yields. Investors borrow in yen at ultra-cheap rates and buy higher-yielding assets elsewhere. It’s one of the quiet pillars of global liquidity. When JGB yields jump, this trade stops being “free money” and starts being dangerous. Funding costs rise. FX hedging costs rise. Risk models panic.

When funding trades break, investors don’t debate fundamentals—they dump risk to unwind leverage. That selling cascades across equities, emerging markets, and crypto. Bitcoin, which trades like a pure bet on global liquidity, absorbs the shock immediately. It falls towards the high $70ks not because anything in crypto changed, but because one of the world’s major liquidity pipes suddenly slammed shut.

Summary & conclusion

This wasn’t a crypto-specific event. It was a liquidity event. Global markets run on cheap funding, and when Japan disrupts the cost of money, everything priced on liquidity reprices. Bitcoin is simply the most honest barometer. When liquidity breaks, it falls first. When liquidity returns, it recovers first.
