Liquidity: The Short-Run Driver of Asset Prices

Core Thesis Liquidity
Key focal point

In the short to medium term, the main driver of asset prices is not “fundamentals” in the textbook sense, but the amount, price and direction of liquidity in the system.

TL;DR

Since 2000 – and decisively since the 2008 crisis – markets have become tightly linked to global liquidity. Central bank balance sheets, credit cycles and carry trades explain more of the big swings in equities, bonds and crypto than traditional valuation models.

Chain of events

In the dot-com era, cheap credit and loose conditions fuelled a classic equity bubble. When liquidity was withdrawn and rates rose, the air came out. The pattern repeated into 2008, but with leverage concentrated in housing and shadow banking rather than tech stocks.

Post-2008, central banks crossed a line: QE, near-zero rates and explicit backstopping of markets. The result was a regime where risk assets became a derivative of central bank balance sheets and dollar funding conditions. Whenever liquidity surged, valuations did too. Whenever liquidity tightened, everything sold off together.

Crypto simply joined this ecosystem with higher beta. Bitcoin and other major tokens now trade less like “uncorrelated alternatives” and more like leveraged plays on global dollar, euro and yen liquidity.

Summary & conclusion

Demographics set the slow backdrop; liquidity decides how violently markets oscillate around it. Watching global liquidity – central bank balance sheets, credit growth, carry trades and fiscal flows – is, in my view, the most useful single lens for understanding where asset prices are heading over the next 6–24 months.

Devine Revaluations exists to track that interaction: long-run demographic pressure and short-run liquidity waves, and what they imply for portfolios and policy.
